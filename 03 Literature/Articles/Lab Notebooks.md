# Lab Notebooks

> [!abstract] Summary
> Keep a lab notebook in the tradition of the practical sciences. Doing so can help you stay on top of your software work and keep the complexities of your day to day manageable by outsourcing the burden of memory and the record of your efforts into writing.
## How

1. **Always pen.** Don't erase any writing, cross it out.
2. **Always during.** Write as your are working each day. What the problem is, what I'm going to do, and what the expected results are.
3. **Always forward.** Never edit past writing. If the writing on day 1 is wrong make the correction on day 2. Only go back to day 1 to point to the correction from day 2.
4. **Table of contents.** First page should work like a Table of Contents (ToC), a few word summarising each section. Focus these summaries to give answer on what you were doing and why.
5. **Keep a habit.** Start each day reading the previous day's notes. End the day reading the day's notes and add to the ToC.
6. **Summarise when necessary.** If a week's worth of notes begins to get messy and hard to follow. Take time to summarise all those notes explaining your learnings and where you are. Flag it explicitly in the ToC.
7. **Store safely.** At the end of a project, label the notebook and store it safely. Focus on making it easy to find and retrieve.

> [!quote]
> It’s a disciplined record of what you do and think each day.

> [!quote]
> One of the hardest things about software engineering is that, while it’s often possible to understand the code you committed, it’s usually impossible to see all the things you tried first, that didn’t work. This is a trap for other developers, and even your future self, where a seemingly better solution gets tried again and again, and each time the same problem arises. But since the failure never gets committed, no record of the attempt is left. ... A lab notebook takes that kind of information _out_ of your memory and puts it somewhere safe and permanent.
